## Visualisation memorability

- Core question: What makes a visualisation memorable?
- Common chart types are generally less memorable than more unique visual forms.
- Visuals with more colour and more human‑recognisable objects tend to be more memorable.
- In the referenced study, memorability was tested via a game:
  - Participants saw images for 2–3 seconds.
  - They clicked if an image repeated.

**Open questions to self**

- Does a visualisation at work need to be memorable, or primarily clear and easy to read?
- Is “more clutter” acceptable if it improves memorability, or does it hurt understanding?

## Data, information, knowledge, wisdom

- Data = facts (numbers, words, images, sound, videos) – lowest level of abstraction.
- Flow: **data → information → knowledge → wisdom**.
- Data quality = trustworthiness of the data.

**Typical data sources**

- Business processes: CRM, SAP, Salesforce, other transactional systems.
- Social media platforms.
- IoT / machines: smart devices and sensors.

**Storage**

- Data is often stored in a central data warehouse and shared across the organisation.

## Data types and storage technologies

- Categorical data:
  - **Nominal** – categories without order (e.g., area of living).
  - **Ordinal** – categories with order (e.g., grad, postgrad).

**Recommended textbook for preprocessing**

- Sharda, R., Delen, D., & Turban, E. (2024). *Business Intelligence, Analytics, Data Science, and AI: A Managerial Perspective* (5th ed.). Pearson.

**Database / storage models**

- Hierarchical – older structure, less flexible.
- Relational – widely used in modern organisations (entity‑relationship model).
- Non‑relational – NoSQL, Apache Spark, Hadoop, used for large‑scale or unstructured data.
